The right to walk

A new path forward for everyone on the streets

Embracing femininity and walking on the streets today is layered, urgent, and complex. There needs to be a safe space for reflection and discussion today. What are the movements, encounters, feelings, and experiences when walking in high heels in public space during the day and night? This site archives a series of walks in high heels from January to August 2023. This work dives into the joys, dreams, pains and discomforts of being feminine and walking in high heels and opens those experiences to an audience during Dutch Design Week. The goal with this work is to create a platform and space where women and feminine people can freely walk on the streets no matter the time of day.

1
Respect everyone, no matter who they are, no matter where they come from, and no matter the time of day. Everyone can access public space, and these spaces can be encouraged to be traversed by everyone.

2
Avoid wolf whistling, catcalling, touching, chasing or following someone.

3
Let women and feminine people not be punished or intimidated by going for a walk.

4
In the Netherlands, you are able to wear what you want on the streets regardless of what people think.¹

5
When a woman or feminine person walks on the streets, they are just walking. The English language is full of words and phrases that sexualize women’s walking. Some terms include streetwalkers, women of the streets, and public women (while phrases such as a public man or man of the streets have different meanings). A woman can be said to be strolling, roaming, wandering, straying—all terms that imply that women’s travel is inevitably sexual or that her sexuality is transgressive when she travels.²

6
Even if a woman or feminine person dresses provocatively, there needs to be baseline respect in public space.
